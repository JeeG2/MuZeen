MAMMUTH:

The Mammut dell'Aquila is once again visible to the public in the east bastion of the sixteenth- century Castle, the historic seat of the National Museum of Abruzzo. The last official publication on the Mammut, in 1985, was the second of the didactic notebooks produced by the then Superintendence for architectural, artistic and historical environmental heritage for Abruzzo, when the scientific denomination was Elephas meridionalis . After the complex restoration funded by the donation of the Guardia di Finanza , the MiC Regional Secretariat for Abruzzowanted to make available to everyone, in this guide, the new knowledge that has emerged, the path taken in recent years.

RILIVEO CON CORTEO FUNEBRE:

The relief found at Amiternum is certainly one of the most important testimonies concerning the performance of a funeral ceremony. The sculptural apparatus represents a scene of funeral procession organized on two compositional registers which, in reality, have the purpose of giving depth and three-dimensionality to the representation. The characters present on the stone slab are numerous, but identifiable in their tasks: starting from the left, above, the praeficae are visible, specially paid women to artfully demonstrate the pain, singing songs in honor of the deceased; on the lower level eight men (lecticarii) are evident, supporting the coffin, anticipated by the dissignator, who directs the ceremony.

CLOSE BURGONET HELMET:

This is the third close burgonet helmet found by Jamestown Rediscovery archaeologists. Introduced in the early 16th century, the close burgonet helmet was designed to provide full head and face protection while at the same time evoking classical military authority. In Europe, this type of helmet was mostly worn by cavalry. There was no cavalry in early Jamestown, so the helmet was modified to make it useful. These fragments of the helmet, the visor and chin protection element collectively known as a bevor, were found in a James Fort cellar that was filled with trash c. 1617. Removal of these components from the skull piece of the burgonet sacrificed some protection but enabled a colonist to wear the skull piece alone as a lighter helmet with greater visibility and flexibility. The skull piece that belongs to this cabasset helmet has not yet been recovered. These parts of the helmet were found discarded in an open position, and although the helmet has undergone extensive conservation, the artifact is concreted so that the hinges on the sides no longer operate. MADONNA IN TRONO CON BAMBINO E ANGELI:

The table, with a gold background, has a golden frame in the shape of a poly-lobed arch resting on two small columns. Above a marble throne covered with a canopy of red festoons alternated with white coat of arms with a black eagle, is the Virgin wrapped in a large dark mantle with a gold border, above a brown robe. On either side of the throne, two praying Angels, dressed one with a large pink robe, the other green, are facing the naked Child in the Virgin's arms, with three little flowers in hand. On the supper of the throne in Latin capitals: AVE. QUEEN. CELORUM. AVE. DOMUS. The painting was unanimously attributed by critics to Saturnino Gatti for affinity with the Madonna del Rosario from the Aquilana church of San Domenico, noting in the breadth of the forms a Pierfrancescan ancestry mediated through Antoniazzo Romano. The Serra also highlighted numerous references to Fiorenzo di Lorenzo in the brightness of the forms and in the modulation of the drapery.
